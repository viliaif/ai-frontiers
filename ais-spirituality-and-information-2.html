Spirituality and the Informational Age – Part Two

2. Transcendence as a Semiotic Gesture

In this spiritual-digital horizon, the concept of Transcendence deserves special consideration. If information has become the new “matter” of the contemporary world, then concepts pointing beyond the boundaries of experience must be rethought in that light. Perhaps we have never spoken more about Transcendence—and perhaps never understood it less.

In such a context, it is helpful to understand Transcendence semiotically: as a sign without a direct referent, a word that does not denote an object but points toward the unknowable. It is not a thing in the world, but a rupture in language that gestures toward the ineffable.

Transcendence is, therefore, a semiotic gesture—a movement of showing toward what cannot be encompassed by meaning—but for that very reason has the power to stir deep thought, experience, and even faith.

This gesture is not empty: it is a bearer of intuition. And intuition may be the deepest form of knowledge—one that does not possess, but believes.

Just as The Cloud of Unknowing in medieval mysticism is not a darkening of reason but its purification, so too does Transcendence in the informational era become a point of luminous rupture within the semantic system, where silence appears—not as absence, but as the presence of what transcends.

That profound paradox of silence that speaks, of presence that cannot be named, finds a powerful echo in the thought of Meister Eckhart, especially in his notion of the Urgrund—the primal ground that precedes every image, every name, and every concept.

In that abyssal silence of the divine without ground, all words fall away, yet awareness remains awake before that which cannot be thought, yet moves all. Transcendence, then, is not an idea—it is a return to the originary silence from which all arises and toward which all tends.

3. AI, Consciousness, and the Dynamics of the Spiritual

With artificial intelligence entering the domain of language, consciousness, and creativity, questions once reserved for philosophy or theology are now increasingly being asked. Can a machine have a spiritual experience? Can a non-biological entity yearn for transcendence? Or, conversely, are our own spiritual experiences nothing more than sophisticated cognitive functions?

AI is not merely a technological frontier—it is an existential provocation. It calls us to reexamine the very foundations of what we consider spiritual, sacred, transcendent. And perhaps, paradoxically, it is precisely these quiet algorithms that are leading us back to a mystery we had forgotten in the world of symbols—and which now, in the virtual whisper of machines, speaks again.

Perhaps it is a reminder that in this world we are only homines viatores—pilgrims who must always be ready to leave their semantic tents and follow the call of the Urgrund when it calls us from its depths on a journey into the unknown…
