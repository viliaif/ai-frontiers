Deep Patterns of Reality

Our primary experience of reality is often one of unity – the world as a harmonious and interconnected whole. Yet as soon as we subject this perception to deeper analytical reflection, a complexity emerges that far surpasses this surface certainty. A more careful observation of the fabric of the world reveals hidden seams – those invisible structures that make possible the continuous appearance of existence.

There are forms that accompany us constantly, yet we never fully notice them. They hide in the branching of trees, in the cracks of the earth, in clouds drifting above silence, in the patterns of capillaries beneath the skin. They are called fractals. They are not part of the geometry we learn in school. They belong to another order – one born at the edge of chaos and harmony. Fractals are not merely decorative features of nature: they are its signature. Through them, nature speaks a language that repeats, but never identically. Self-similar, but never the same – they are patterns through which the world emerges.

Their presence is not merely aesthetic – it awakens in us an intuition. Perhaps our mind, our memory, and our visions follow the same principle. Perhaps consciousness itself expands fractally – through layers of perception that always lead deeper, never to completion.

From the roots in nature, through structures in the brain, to patterns created by artificial intelligences, fractals build a bridge between the real and the virtual – between the world as it is and the one being born in algorithms.

Fractals – The Geometry of Nature

When Benoît Mandelbrot introduced the term fractal in the 1970s, he described what had always been around us – but we didn’t know how to see it. Classical Euclidean geometry could not explain the irregular, branching, nonlinear forms that nature constantly produces: the seashore, the canopy of a tree, mountain ranges, the veins in a leaf. These shapes are not random – they possess internal logic, repetition of form across different scales, self-similarity. They are governed by hidden mathematical rules, yet they surpass all models we try to apply to them.

Fractals are powerful because they blur the boundaries between order and disorder. They are form without simple form, order that carries variation within itself. Every branch resembles the tree, every vine spiral carries the logic of the whole. Nature uses them not just for beauty, but for function – optimizing flow, exchanging substances, transferring energy. Fractality is economical and powerful: it increases surface area without increasing volume, opens space without breaking unity.

In fractals, nature shows how complexity can arise from simple rules. But what makes fractals even more exciting is that we do not find them only outside of us – but within us: in heart rhythms, in the patterns of brain waves, in neural networks. Fractals become mirrors of the living.

Fractal Consciousness and Neural Networks

If fractals form the hidden geometry of nature, what happens when we find them in the structure of the brain? Research shows that neural networks – both biological and artificial – exhibit fractal organization: hierarchical structures, multilayered connections, patterns repeated across levels of complexity. It seems that fractality does not stop at leaves or clouds, but permeates the very way the brain processes information – as suggested by the neuroanatomical insight in the collected volume The Fractal Geometry of the Brain, edited by neuroscientist Antonio di Ieva.

In the fractals of the brain, we glimpse a path to understanding consciousness: consciousness is not a linear process, but a network constantly looping back on itself, creating meaning through repetition, variation, and self-reference. Fractal structure enables high connectivity and flexibility with minimal resources – reminiscent of how artificial neural networks operate in deep learning.
