Spirituality and the Informational Age

The contemporary age has brought a radical transformation in our relationship to materiality. Information technology—together with the achievements of quantum and nuclear physics—has called into question the classical understanding of matter as a solid, stable, and autonomous foundation of reality. Instead, we increasingly encounter views that interpret the world as a dynamic network of information, relations, and potentialities. In this context, one may speak of the informational dematerialization of reality—a process in which the physical and tangible increasingly give way to the digital, algorithmic, and virtual.

1. Informational Dematerialization and Spiritual Reality

This transition does not only change science and technology, but also the way we reflect on spirituality itself. For in many traditions, the “spiritual” has been defined—explicitly or implicitly—in contrast to the material. If our very understanding of materiality is changing, placing emphasis on the informational dimension of reality, what does that mean for what we call “spiritual”? Is the boundary between them disappearing? Or is a new opportunity emerging for deeper understanding?

The question of the spiritual in the informational age inevitably brings us back to theological approaches that, within the Judeo-Christian tradition, have long expressed a certain reservation toward material forms in speaking about God, as well as in representing Him.

We are thinking here primarily of apophatic theology and iconoclasm—currents within Christian thought that emphasize the limitations of language, symbols, and images in attempting to express that which exceeds every conception.

For the apophatic tradition, God cannot be approached through positive assertions, but primarily through negation—through silence, unknowing, and a humble acceptance of the unknowability of the Absolute.

This theological intuition is deeply rooted in the Old Testament spiritual horizon, where God's name is not spoken, and His likeness is not depicted. The commandment “You shall not make for yourself a graven image” (Ex 20:4), and the strict observance of the unutterability of God's name (YHWH), testify to an early understanding of the sacred as something that eludes all human representation.

In this light, the dematerialization brought by the informational age need not be a threat, but can serve as an analogy for spiritual truths: just as the virtual can be real yet immaterial, so too can the spiritual be real yet inaccessible to the senses.
