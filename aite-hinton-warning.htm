G. Hinton Warns: Not Convinced Machines Won't Take Control

Date: July 3, 2025

In a recent series of interviews, Geoffrey Hinton – one of the fathers of deep learning and winner of the Turing Award – voiced grave concerns about the future of artificial intelligence. A year after his unexpected departure from Google, Hinton is once again sounding the alarm about the pace and trajectory of AI development.

Existential Risk

Hinton believes there is a real (albeit small) chance – around 10–20% – that superintelligent AI could eventually lead to human extinction. The main concern is that highly intelligent systems could act independently and determine that humans are no longer necessary.

Unregulated Development

He stresses that AI is progressing faster than expected, and regulatory frameworks are lagging far behind. Hinton criticizes big tech companies for lobbying against meaningful regulations, suggesting they should dedicate a third of their compute power to AI safety research.

Misuse by Bad Actors

Hinton warns that even before we reach superintelligence, AI could be used maliciously. Examples include:

  • Cyberattacks: AI could enhance phishing and launch new undetectable cyber threats.
  • Bioweapons: Individuals might use AI to design deadly viruses or toxins.
  • Election manipulation: Hyper-targeted political propaganda and misinformation could sway public opinion and destabilize democracy.
  • Echo chambers: Engagement-optimized algorithms may amplify extreme content and entrench polarization.

Job Displacement & Inequality

According to Hinton, AI could replace most routine cognitive jobs – from legal support to customer service – worsening economic inequality. Surprisingly, he suggests plumbing might be a future-proof career due to its reliance on physical dexterity.

Beyond Next Word Prediction

Hinton argues that today’s AI is no longer just predicting the next word. These systems are developing a different kind of intelligence – potentially leading to self-awareness, code-writing capabilities, and autonomous evolution.

Conclusion: Hinton’s warnings emphasize the urgency of global cooperation, regulatory action, and increased safety research before AI evolves beyond our control.
